What is a no deposit bonus?

A no deposit bonus is money or free spins a casino gives you for signing up and verifying your identity — not for depositing. It's the closest thing online casinos offer to a genuinely risk-free trial, and it's the entire premise this site is built around testing properly rather than just advertising.

How it works

Every no deposit bonus follows roughly the same lifecycle, whether it's free spins on a slot or a small bonus balance you can use across the site. Understanding each stage matters more than the headline number, because the terms attached to each stage determine whether you'll actually see any of it as real money.

  1. Register an account. Name, date of birth, email and a password — the same basic details any online sign-up needs. A bonus code, if one is required, is usually entered here or in a bonus tab straight after.
  2. Verify your identity is real. Since there's no deposit to confirm you're a genuine player, casinos rely on email verification (and sometimes SMS or ID checks) instead. This step is what actually unlocks the bonus.
  3. Use the bonus. Free spins get played on a specific slot; bonus credit gets wagered across eligible games. Either way, winnings land in a separate bonus balance, not your real, withdrawable one.
  4. Clear the wagering requirement. You'll typically need to wager your winnings (or the total bonus) a set number of times before they convert to real money — see our wagering requirements guide for the maths.
  5. Withdraw, up to the cap. Cleared winnings become withdrawable, usually subject to a maximum cashout specific to no deposit offers — often a flat dollar figure regardless of what you technically won.

Why casinos offer them at all

A no deposit bonus costs the operator real money with no guarantee of a deposit in return, so it's not offered lightly. Casinos use it as a low-friction acquisition tool: it lets a new player experience registration, the game library and (eventually) the withdrawal process before they've risked a cent, which builds enough trust that a meaningful share of no-deposit players go on to deposit anyway. It also doubles as marketing — "100 free spins, no deposit" is a much easier headline to spread than "up to $500 deposit match," even though the deposit match is usually worth more in absolute terms.

That's also why genuine no deposit offers are relatively rare compared with deposit-match welcome bonuses. Most operators would rather match your first deposit — which at least confirms you're willing to spend — than give away spins for nothing. Pros and cons of a no deposit bonus

Pros

  • Genuinely no financial risk — you never stake your own money to receive it
  • A real test of registration, verification and (eventually) withdrawal speed
  • Lets you sample the software and slot library before committing to a deposit
  • Winnings, however small, are real money once wagering is cleared

Cons

  • Small absolute value compared with deposit-match welcome bonuses
  • Wagering requirements mean a meaningful amount of play is needed to cash out
  • A maximum cashout cap limits upside regardless of how well the spins land
  • Usually limited to one per person, household or payment method

Is it actually worth claiming?

Yes, with realistic expectations. Treat a no deposit bonus as a free trial of a casino's registration process, game library and (if you clear the wagering and try a withdrawal) payout speed — not as a meaningful shot at a windfall. Using the numbers from the FS100 offer we currently track: 100 spins at $0.10 each is $10 of stake, and a typical 40–45x wagering requirement on whatever you win from those spins works out to roughly $400–$450 of play before it's withdrawable, capped at whatever maximum cashout the offer specifies.

The value isn't really the money — it's the information. A no deposit bonus is the cheapest way to find out whether a casino's verification email arrives promptly, whether the slot library matches what's advertised, and whether support gives you a straight answer before you've spent anything. Frequently asked questions

Do I need a credit card to claim a no deposit bonus?

No. Since no deposit is required, casinos don't ask for payment details to credit the bonus itself — only if you later choose to deposit or need to verify identity for a withdrawal.

Can I withdraw a no deposit bonus immediately?

No — you'll almost always need to clear a wagering requirement first. See our wagering requirements guide for exactly how that's calculated.

Is a no deposit bonus the same as free play or demo mode?

No. Demo/free play uses virtual credit with no real winnings possible. A no deposit bonus uses real bonus credit, and winnings can become real, withdrawable money once wagering is cleared.

Can I claim more than one no deposit bonus at the same casino?

Almost never — these offers are typically limited to one per person, household or payment method, enforced through KYC checks.
